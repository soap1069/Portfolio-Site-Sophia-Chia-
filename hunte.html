HUNTE

A scavenger hunt app for meeting friends through quirky adventures.

Project Overview

Purpose

I designed HUNTE for my keystone project in the CareerFoundry UX design course. I am inspired by the social opportunities that technology can offer, so working on a social scavenger hunt app with the aim of connecting people from different paths of life was a very exciting project for me. I also decided to expand more on the app beyond the project scope as I was encouraged by the feedback from the testers that they would love to use the app.

Approach

1 Understand

Market & Competition

Many competitors occupy the scavenger hunt app market. Most are catered to businesses, schools, or events where users can build their own hunts. They are also not catered to helping users seek people to play with, but assume an already established relationship among users that hunt together. Few receive high ratings or have an active marketing strategy. I looked deeper into Let’s Roam and Actionbound, two of the more dominant apps in the app store in terms of popularity and ratings, to benchmark features and design.

User Research

Having understood the market and competitor products better, I wanted to understand if playing scavenger hunts with players from an app was appealing, and what preferences people had around such games. I conducted interviews with potential users in order to better understand user needs and thoughts around travel and scavenger hunt apps and clustered the insights using affinity mapping. 6 main themes were clustered: travel needs, social needs, gaming format, tasks & content, safety & trust, and experience and knowledge.

Notable points:

  1. Several interviewees spoke about the importance of using google maps for navigation --> It would be helpful to have maps to locate hunts and tasks.
  2. Interviewees stressed about coordination and communication if there are too many people in a hunt --> It is better to keep groups smaller. And assistive features such as group chats can be provided.
  3. Interviewees liked the idea of meeting people from different cultures & want to meet people with a similar traveling schedule and/or similar interests. --> It could be designed that users can easily identify other players by country or language.

2 Empathize

User personas

To empathize better with the user throughout the design process, I created user personas based on the interview participants I had met with. There are 2 target personas:

Erik - A friendly introvert who seeks fun challenges and enjoys meeting people via online channels so he can filter for those who have the same interests.

Tom - A curious and trendy person who travels a lot and likes to post about his unique and fun cultural experiences on social media.

User journeys

I created user journeys based on the personas in order to visualize the process the user would go through to accomplish his or her goal. This helped me better understand the pain points and emotions of the user in context. Erik is bored at home and is looking for someone to hand out and do something out of the ordinary with. Tom is traveling alone and wants to explore the city in an unconventional way, and ideally with a local or traveler from another culture or nationality. I explore the tasks, thoughts, feelings that the two personas would go through to eventually play a scavenger hunt with new friends.

Task analysis and user flow

I then created user flows based on my users objective to clarify the main pages and screens the user would go through to achieve his/her goal. A task analysis is also conducted for each objective to help determine what tasks are required to complete the goal and define the overall flow and experience of the user.

3 Ideate

Wireframes & prototype

Having the research and user analysis under my belt, I gathered my learnings and went to the ideation process, which involved a lot of iterative sketching. The result were low fidelity wireframes that could already be used to collect feedback and identify areas for refinement. After several design iterations involving rounds of feedback, I created mid-fidelity wireframes, which were then used for user testing. The sketches and low-fidelity wireframes were created with pencil and paper, the mid-fidelity wireframe was created with Balsamiq and the mid-fidelity prototype was created with Sketch.

4 Test

Usability testing

Usability testing allowed me to identify issues and make changes quickly to my app. A total of 6 test participants were recruited for the first round of testing. Test participants were in their 20s or 30s and have traveled at least twice a year to different countries, and were similar to my target personas. All test participants took an in-person moderated test, which gave me the opportunity to also observe their facial and body expressions.

TEST OBJECTIVES

General navigation

‍Do users understand navigation menu? Can users easily navigate from one part of the app to another? Are there any frictions in navigation?

‍Find a hunt

‍Can users successfully move through the flow of finding and joining a hunt? Do users understand what is the relationship between a hunt and a meetup?

‍Manage friend list

‍Can users add a player as friend? Do they know how to see if they have any friend requests?

‍Edit profile

‍Do users know where to go to edit their profile? Do they know how to change their profile image?

TEST OBJECTIVES

Usability issues were rated on the Nielsen Scale (0 = no issue, 4 = imperative to fix)

Results analysis

RAINBOW SPREAD SHEET

I used a rainbow spread sheet to organize my findings from the usability tests. Errors, observations, positive and negative quotes were listed, tallied and prioritized. Based on this, I also came up with possible solutions and next steps to adjust my existing app design.

ISSUES & SOLUTION

5 discovered issues were prioritized for design adjustments:

1. Users couldn't find the profile page and assumed that it is in settings

Solution: Add "Go to profile" in the menu where the profile page can be accessed

2. Users wanted to see the hunts on a map and to see how many games or hunts were in the area.

Solution: Add a map view to the home page so users can see where all the hunts are located

3. Users did not know if they should start with Find hunts or Find players.

Solution: Place Find players in the menu so the home page is only about Find hunts

4. Users did not know what hunt modes mean.

Solution: Provide a description of what each hunt modes mean when you tap on it

5. Users did not know what the difference between Hunt maps and Hunt meetup is.

Solution: Change Hunt meetup to Upcoming games so it is more obvious that the game is an instance on the Hunt map.

5 Refine

Style guide

After making the changes to the app based on user feedback, it was time to refine the UI. I created a style guide for the app so there is a consistent style applied for all the design assets and elements. I chose bold and high contrast colors and playful font because I want to give an exciting and fun mood to the app.

Final product design

Though the project is completed for the UX course, there are still parts of the app that I would like to add and change. I am very happy to have received lots of feedback from testers as well as peer UX designers, which have enabled me to make significant changes to the app since its first draft and given me the confidence that the usability has improved a lot.
